Environment

With a dementia disease, the brain damage can make it more strenuous to understand and interpret one’s surroundings. For example, a person with dementia might be more sensitive to different sounds.

It is therefore important to limit the number of different stimuli, and create a calm and welcoming atmosphere. By adapting the surroundings for persons with dementia, it will be easier for them to feel secure and to find their way around.

Remember that you are also a part of the environment. Your way of moving and facial expressions are significant and can be interpreted as both positive and negative. It is important to try to convey a sense of security and friendliness, when communicating with persons with dementia.

- Minimize sounds. A door that slams shut can seem frightening. A dishwashing machine, a fan or sounds from an open window can also be disturbing. Think about the way in which you and your colleagues talk to each other.

- Limit visual stimuli. Only lay the table with what is necessary for the meal. Don’t put more cutlery, glass or plates on the table than needed. Serve one dish at a time. Remember to use contrasting colours so that it will be easier to see and interpret, for example, a white fish dish on a coloured plate.

- Explain in simple words what is going to happen, for example: “It’s now twelve o’clock and time for lunch.” Don’t ask questions of fact. Instead ask if a person is hungry and show the food or a sandwich.

- If someone is anxious, say “I’ll take care of this, don’t you worry about it”. Remember that your body language often reveals if you are irritated or stressed.

- Avoid sudden moves or speaking too quickly, or above a person’s head. Use simple language.
